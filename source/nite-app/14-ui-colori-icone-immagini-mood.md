# Color, Icons, Images, Mood, and Tone of Voice

This file collects principles of mood and style in UI design: color as a system, palettes, icons, images, expressive typography, and visual tone of voice.

## Color as a design choice

Color is not decoration, personal taste, or an instinctive response. It is a design, systemic, and intentional choice.

Color:

- communicates;
- influences emotions and behavior, often below the level of awareness;
- is one of the first perceptions of the brand;
- generates emotional associations;
- can influence 60-80% of the choice of a product according to the reference cited in the materials.

## Cultural trap

Simple associations such as "blue = trust" or "red = passion" are only partially true and can be dangerous.

The meaning of colors is:

- influenced by biology;
- conditioned by culture;
- dependent on context;
- built in relation to other colors.

Correct question: "What does this color mean here, for this audience, in this category?"

## Roles of color in UI and brand

- brand coherence;
- brand differentiation;
- strengthening readability;
- strengthening a product;
- suggesting an action.

## Color as perception

Color is visual perception generated by the nerve signals that the retina's photoreceptors send to the brain when they absorb electromagnetic radiation of certain wavelengths and intensities.

It does not exist as an autonomous property: it exists as a perceptual experience.

## Color models

| Model | Use | Description |
| --- | --- | --- |
| RGB | Digital | Red, green, blue, values 0-255. |
| HEX | Digital | Compact translation of RGB, for example `#66C2FF`. |
| CMYK | Print | Cyan, magenta, yellow, black. |
| HSB | Digital | Hue, Saturation, Brightness. |

## HSB

- `Hue`: hue, point on the color wheel from 0° to 360°.
- `Saturation`: amount of gray compared with the hue; 0% is gray, 100% is full saturation.
- `Brightness`: relative brightness; 0% black, 100% maximum brightness of the hue.

## Psychology of color

Color is the first visual language the brain interprets. It acts before rationality, activating unconscious emotional and perceptual responses.

Palette choice influences:

- style;
- atmosphere;
- look & feel;
- memorability;
- emotions;
- product perception.

## Colors and UI conventions

### Blue

Very widespread in digital products, especially tech and SaaS. In Europe it is historically associated with sky, the divine, stability, order, and reliability. In contemporary use it communicates calm, safety, control, and reliability. It is frequent in corporate, technology, finance, insurance, healthcare, and institutions.

### Green

The human eye distinguishes many shades of green. It is suited to states, levels, and progressions. It works for non-aggressive feedback. If overused, it becomes visual noise. In UI it does not mean positive in itself: it becomes so by convention and coherent use.

### Red

Very visually salient: urgent, critical, impossible to ignore. In UI it identifies errors, critical states, alerts, warnings, and definitive actions such as delete, remove, reset. If used too much, it generates stress, reduces readability, and loses semantic meaning.

### Yellow

High visibility, less aggressive than red. It communicates that something deserves attention. It should be balanced with neutrals. It works in light or pastel tones as a background and in more saturated tones as a specific accent. Recommended use: non-critical warnings, temporary highlights, accents.

## Color palette

A palette is a designed color system, meaning a set of related colors that guarantee visual coherence over time and across touchpoints.

It is not only a set generated by a tool: it governs the brand's visual behavior.

## Color wheel

The color wheel represents relationships between colors. It is used to:

- create harmonies;
- understand contrasts and pairings;
- support aesthetic and functional choices.

The 12-color RYB wheel is useful educationally, but in UI and visual design RGB and HSL/HSB are mainly used.

## Types of palettes

| Palette | Description | Warnings |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Monochromatic | Shades of a single color, including black and white. | Safe but with lower contrast. |
| Analogous | Colors close to each other on the color wheel. | Good starting point, but limited contrast. |
| Complementary | Colors opposite each other on the wheel. | High contrast; avoid clashing colors. |
| Triadic | Three colors in an equilateral triangle. | Colorful and playful; define primary, secondary, and tertiary. |
| Split-complementary | Primary + two colors near the complement. | Safe, but contrast must be tested. |
| Rectangular | Four colors in a rectangle. | Choose one primary and use the others as accents. |
| Square | Four complementary colors in a square. | Use one primary and limit the others. |

Recommended ratio for triadic: 60% primary, 30% secondary, 10% tertiary.

## Grays connected to the primary color

When defining a primary color, it can be useful to create grays with a hint of the primary color:

- dark gray: original saturation -30, original brightness -30;
- medium gray: saturation 10-15, brightness 95-100;
- light gray: saturation 5-10, brightness 95-100.

This makes the system more refined and coherent.

## Icons

An icon is a small visual pictogram that represents:

- an action, for example save or search;
- a function;
- a state, for example active, inactive, error, success.

Icons reduce cognitive load and occupied space.

## Icon comprehensibility

The meaning of an icon is not always universal. Some are linked to real objects, others are symbolic or abstract, often learned by convention.

Rules:

- the simpler the icon, the more understandable it is;
- include labels when the meaning is not universally shared;
- for older, less experienced, or disabled targets, keep labels;
- for primary navigation, back, or hamburger menu, the label can be omitted only if the context allows it.

## Icon style

All product icons must have a coherent style.

Factors:

- full shape or outline;
- details, shadows, or multiple colors;
- construction grid;
- roundness;
- stroke width;
- level of detail;
- readability at small sizes;
- semantic recognizability.

### Construction grid

The grid is the icon's DNA: it guarantees proportions, readability, negative spaces, and coherence. Shapes, curves, and angles are not random, but follow steps and multiples.

### Roundness and tone

- rounded corners: friendlier and more consumer-oriented;
- sharp or acute corners: more serious/professional;
- friendly outline: thicker stroke;
- professional/minimal: thinner stroke.

### Legibility and readability of icons

- `Legibility`: ease of distinguishing shape and details.
- `Recognizability`: speed with which the user interprets meaning and purpose.

Simple, solid shapes with rounded corners work well at many scales. Complex icons, thin outlines, or very detailed icons lose legibility when resized.

## Bounding box

The bounding box is the safe area around the icon. It is used to balance irregular shapes, standardize spacing, support accessibility, and maintain coherence.

## Images

Images are not decoration: they communicate and differentiate the product.

Best practices:

1. Choose images relevant to the context and value proposition.
2. Always aim for focus: the user should not have to look for meaning.
3. Avoid stock or fake photos: they become visual noise.
4. Use different media: photographs for specificity, illustrations for concepts and metaphors.
5. Choose images relevant also for accessibility and screen readers.

## Background photos and readability

Problem: text over photos can become hard to read.

Solutions:

- dark or light overlay;
- black with 50-80% opacity for many photos;
- overlay with primary color mixed with dark gray for coherence;
- gradient overlay if the content occupies only one area;
- hard overlay with opaque rectangle under text, suited to labels and icons but more impactful with headings.

If images are dynamic or user-generated, readability is not predictable: compromises and resilient systems are needed.

## Typography as tone of voice

Before colors and images, typography gives voice to the interface. Choosing a typeface means defining how the product speaks to the user.

A typeface can communicate personality:

- elegant, calm, timeless, sophisticated;
- friendly, playful, genuine, open;
- direct, bold, strong, sincere.

It does not mean using typefaces with forced personality. The choice must better convey the visual message.

## Combining typefaces

Combining two typefaces is complex and requires experience.

Approaches:

- contrast, for example Futura + Baskerville;
- harmony, for example Helvetica + DIN Alternate;
- a single family with different weights and styles;
- super family, meaning families designed together, for example Merriweather + Merriweather Sans or Roboto + Roboto Slab.

A single family is often enough to maintain consistency.
